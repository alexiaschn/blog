A Paradigmatic issue

LLMs are based on the distributional hypothesis (Harris 1981):

You shall know a word by the company it keeps. — Firth (1962)

vs. for instance generative linguistics:

Colorless green ideas sleep furiously — Chomsky (1957)

In the context of writing and information retrieval: is the most probable output the most relevant output?

Discoverability/Découvrabilité

Findability/Trouvabilité

Serendipity/Trouvaille : ‘browsing’ is a 4 dimensions process (Rice, McCreadie, and Chang 2001) : 1. the act of scanning; 2. the presence or absence of purpose; 3. the specificity of search outcomes or goals; 4. and knowledge about the resource and object sought.

Modelisation of serendipity with reflexive intent (Makri and Blandford 2012)

Serendipity is a process rather than a goal, and this process implies necessarily a selection step based on complex cognitive connexions between past and present information.

Putting serendipity at the forefront of dedicated research and writing tools can be a way to 1. reconnect digital tools and the internet to its roots (open exploration based on a network of unrelated data) 2. allow human agency to be exercised and a support for ai litteracy.

Main goals

  • Expliciting the biases, improving on AI- and digital- literacy.
  • Suggesting epistemic alternatives : e.g. valorisation of serendipity and corrections as an engagement strategy vs. personalization of interactions.
  • Developping tools and functionnality materialising this theoretical standpoint :
    1. Semantic enriching with LOD embedded in the text : text editor Stylo
    2. Alternative exploration of large corpora on qualitative aspects (e.g. citation dis/agreement) : journals, érudit, OpenEdition, Isidore.
    3. Querying strategy using semantic structured data (via IEML) as opposed to natural language : Isidore.
